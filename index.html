Sharks. Their Importance and the Risk of Extinction.

For decades, sharks have been portrayed as villains of the sea. Since the release of Jaws in 1975, countless films have depicted great whites and other species as merciless man-eaters. In reality, however, humans are not part of a shark’s natural diet.

There are over 465 known species of sharks, ranging from the whale shark—which feeds primarily on plankton—to powerful predators like the great white. Most sharks feed on fish, crustaceans, mollusks, krill, marine mammals, and occasionally other sharks. Their role at the top of the food chain helps regulate marine ecosystems, keeping populations of prey species in balance.

While sharks are formidable hunters, they are not out to devour humans. Sharks are naturally curious: when they approach you, it’s often to investigate rather than to attack. In many recorded “shark bites,” a shark takes a quick exploratory nip—and upon realizing you’re not its usual prey—immediately retreats. True, shark bites can be dangerous, but fatal attacks on humans are extremely rare.

If you ever find yourself sharing the water with a shark, consider these safety tips:

  • Stay low in the water. Sharks often mistake silhouettes at the surface for seals or other marine mammals.
  • Avoid erratic movements. Smooth, steady motions reduce curiosity-driven approaches.
  • Don’t provoke. Never corner or harass a shark; give it space to swim away.

Despite their critical ecological role, shark populations worldwide are in decline. Two major threats are:

  1. Overfishing for fins. Millions of sharks are caught each year, their fins removed, and bodies discarded at sea.
  2. By-catch and habitat degradation. Sharks get unintentionally trapped in fishing gear, and pollution further poisons their environment.

Protecting sharks benefits the entire ocean. As apex predators, they help clear dead or weak marine life, contributing to healthier reefs and fisheries. They’re also valuable to science—research into shark immune systems has led to advances in cancer therapy and tissue regeneration.

How You Can Help

  • Donate: Even $5 can support organizations that remove ocean debris and fund shark-safe fishing practices.
  • Sign petitions: Back international bans on shark finning and campaigns for marine protected areas.
  • Spread awareness: Challenge shark myths and share the truth about their importance.

By working together, we can ensure that future generations will continue to marvel at—and benefit from—the world’s most graceful and vital marine predators.
